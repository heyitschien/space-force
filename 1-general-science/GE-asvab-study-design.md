Goal: help you answer not only repeated questions, but also new wording of the same core concepts.

---

## 1) What this subtest is really testing

General Science mostly tests whether you can do three things quickly:

1. **Recognize the concept bucket** (space, life, atoms, Earth, measurement)
2. **Match the exact definition** (not a near-true choice)
3. **Avoid trap substitutions** (wrong unit, swapped term, wrong system)

If you master those 3 skills, you can handle most unseen General Science items.

## 3) Concept coverage extracted from practice tests

### A) Astronomy & Space

Repeated patterns:

- Planet order facts (for example, 4th from sun = Mars)
- Largest planet (Jupiter), rings count (4 planets)
- Closest star besides sun (Proxima Centauri)
- Largest moon in solar system (Ganymede)
- Eclipse distinction (moon in Earth shadow = lunar eclipse)
- Blue moon vocabulary
- Basic astronomy profession vocabulary (astronomer)

### B) Biology & Human Body

Repeated patterns:

- Blood roles (RBC vs WBC vs antibodies/infection)
- Body systems (nervous, digestive, respiratory, circulatory)
- Cell concepts (nucleus, protoplasm, water in cells)
- Digestion starts in mouth
- Joint type (fixed joints)
- Classification (kingdom broadest, herbivore/carnivore)
- Genetics vocabulary (gene)
- Food roles (carbs = energy; protein source exceptions)
- Food chain producer/consumer roles

### C) Chemistry & Atoms

Repeated patterns:

- Electron charge (negative)
- Atomic number = number of protons
- Atom is smallest unit retaining element behavior
- Element vs molecule/mixture confusion traps
- Oxidation wording (electron loss in common test framing)
- Acid property (sour taste, pH below 7)
- Chlorophyll/photosynthesis vocabulary

### D) Earth Science Fundamentals

Repeated patterns:

- Earth layers (crust as surface/upper solid layer)
- Biosphere term (where life exists)
- Igneous rock identification (pumice)
- Biome recognition (tropical rainforest: high rainfall + high temp)

### E) Measurement & Physics Constants

Repeated patterns:

- Metric conversion (1 meter = 100 centimeters; 1 kilometer = 1000 meters)
- Celsius boiling point of water (100 C)
- Speed of light (about 186,000 miles/second)
- Velocity vs speed vocabulary
- Energy/work/power relationship and units (energy = ability to do work; power = watts)
- Melting point definition
- Newton law framing (force and acceleration relationship)

## 5) Core principles (so you can answer new wording)

### Astronomy & Space

- Planet facts are often tested as ranking/order/identity
- Moon/eclipse items test event definitions, not math
- "Closest star" question usually excludes the sun explicitly

### Biology & Human Body

- Function questions dominate (what organ/cell/system does what)
- Distinctions beat rote memorization (RBC vs WBC, system A vs system B)
- Nutrition items often ask for "EXCEPT" or "not a major source"

### Chemistry & Atoms

- Subatomic roles are high frequency: proton identity vs electron charge
- "Smallest unit" wording separates atom from molecule/compound/mixture
- Acid/base facts often test basic property recognition

### Earth Science

- Layer/location questions are common (surface, crust, biosphere)
- Rock and biome questions are usually one-step concept recognition

### Measurement & Physics

- Unit traps are common (second vs minute vs hour)
- Vocabulary precision matters: speed vs velocity, energy vs power

## 6) Trap-choice patterns you should expect

1. **Near-true units**  
   Example: light speed per hour/minute instead of per second.

2. **Term swapping**  
   Example: electrons vs protons for atomic number.

3. **System swapping**  
   Example: spinal cord put under circulatory instead of nervous system.

4. **Category confusion**  
   Example: element vs molecule vs mixture.

5. **EXCEPT/NOT wording**  
   Example: protein source questions where apples are the trap.

Rule: answer by exact definition first, then check unit and category.

---

## 7) Definition -> Contrast -> Example map (for app cards)

Use this schema for every concept card:

- **Definition**: what it is
- **Contrast**: what it is not
- **Example**: one concrete instance
- **Memory anchor**: short sticky phrase

Example:

- Definition: Atomic number is number of protons.
- Contrast: Not electrons, not periodic table location itself.
- Example: Element identity changes when proton count changes.
- Anchor: "Protons define identity."

## 8) 60-second drill (cross-test)

Answer without notes:

1. Largest planet?
2. Fourth planet from sun?
3. Closest star besides sun?
4. Rings on how many planets?
5. Electron charge?
6. Atomic number is based on what?
7. 1 meter equals how many centimeters?
8. Digestion starts where?
9. Which blood cell carries oxygen?
10. Earth's surface layer?

Target: 9/10+ in under 60 seconds.

## 9) Mixed transfer drill (build for app quiz mode)

Build 15-question mixed sets with this ratio:

- 3 Astronomy
- 4 Biology
- 3 Chemistry/Atoms
- 2 Earth Science
- 3 Measurement/Physics

Question types to rotate:

- Direct definition
- Compare/contrast
- "Which is NOT..."
- Unit check
- Real-world mini scenario

This prevents memorizing only one wording.

## 10) High-retention 4-day cycle

### Day 1 (40 min)

- Study sections 2-6.
- Do 60-second drill twice.
- Mark weak concepts.

### Day 2 (25 min)

- Only weak concepts: Definition/Contrast/Example.
- One 15-question mixed set.

### Day 3 (25 min)

- Do a timed 25-question General Science set (11 minutes).
- Log each miss by concept bucket.

### Day 4 (20 min)

- Re-study only missed buckets.
- Final 60-second drill (goal 10/10).

## 11) What "high-yield" means in this guide

In this guide, "must-know/high-yield" means:

- Repeated directly in the practice tests, or
- Core foundational science fact repeatedly used to build trap choices.

So it is **test-grounded plus transfer-grounded**, not random trivia.

---

## 12) Final exam execution rule

For each question:

1. Identify bucket in 1 second.
2. Recall definition in 2-4 seconds.
3. Eliminate trap answers by unit/system/category.
4. Choose and move on.

Speed + concept precision is the winning combination for General Science.
