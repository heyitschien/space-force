<a id="big-idea"></a>

# 1. BIG IDEA 🎯

## Word problems are translation problems

ASVAB word problems are usually not testing advanced math.

They are testing whether you can take a sentence like:

> “One number is 8 more than the other”

and convert it into:

[
y = x + 8
]

That is the real skill.

Your job is always:

1. **Identify what is being asked**
2. **Define the unknown**
3. **Translate each sentence into math**
4. **Solve**
5. **Check whether the answer makes real-world sense** (remainder? round up? CBD?)

---

<a id="word-problem-attack-system"></a>

# 2. THE MASTER PROCESS 🛠️

Use this process every time.

## Word Problem Attack System

**Core flow (one glance before you calculate):**

```text
  ASK          →   DEFINE x    →   TRANSLATE    →   SOLVE    →   INTERPRET
(what want?)      (unknown)        (words→math)      (compute)   (r? ⌈·⌉? CBD?)
```

> **✅ Check:** “Interpret” is not optional on AR — the ASVAB often cares whether you return the **remainder**, the **next whole bus/trip**, or **cannot be determined**.

### Step A — Ask: “What is the unknown?”

What are they asking for?

* smaller number?
* number of buses?
* number in the last room?
* total cost?
* number of trips?
* angle measure?
* speed?
* time?

Name the unknown clearly.

### Step B — Define a variable

Let a variable represent the unknown.

Examples:

* Let (x) = smaller number
* Let (x) = number of rooms fully filled
* Let (x) = number of hours
* Let (x) = cost per item

If you define the wrong thing carelessly, the setup gets messy.

### Step C — Translate sentence by sentence

Turn words into equations.

Example: “The sum of two numbers is 70. One number is 8 more than the other.”

* sum → add
* is → equals
* one number is 8 more than the other → larger = smaller + 8

So:

[
x + (x+8) = 70
]

### Step D — Solve carefully

Now it becomes normal algebra or arithmetic.

### Step E — Check the meaning of the answer

Sometimes you must **round up**, sometimes you use the **remainder**, sometimes the answer is **not enough information**, sometimes the raw decimal is not realistic.

Example: 104 people, plane holds 42.

[
104 \div 42 = 2.47...
]

You cannot make **0.47 of a trip**. The real answer is **3 trips**.

<a id="pattern-recognition-map"></a>

# 3. PATTERN RECOGNITION MAP 🗺️

Four “slots” cover most translation-first items. **Pick the row before you crunch numbers.**

| Cue | Pattern | Situation | Key trick |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 🔢 | **Type 1 — Number relationships** | Sum/difference/twice/three times a number | Define simpler unknown; build (x) and (x ± k) or (kx ± m) |
| 🪑 | **Type 2 — Grouping / remainder** | People into rooms, rows, equal groups | **Divide**; “how many in the *partial* group?” → **remainder** |
| 🚌 | **Type 3 — Capacity / round up** | Buses, trips, vans, boxes, “how many needed” | **Divide**; any positive remainder → **quotient + 1** |
| ❓ | **Type 4 — CBD / ambiguous** | Missing label, unnamed geometry piece | If the **target is under-specified**, answer **cannot be determined** |

<a id="pattern-type-1--number-relationships"></a>

# 4. DEEP DIVE — THE FOUR TYPES 👀

---

## Type 1 — Number relationships 🔢

### Visual

Smaller number = (x), larger = (x + 8). If their sum is 70:

[
x + (x+8)=70
]

### Key trick / translation

* “Sum of two numbers” → add the expressions.
* “One is 8 more than the other” → larger = smaller + 8 (define **smaller first** when you can).
* Also: twice a number, three times a number, consecutive integers — same habit: **one equation per sentence**.

### Worked micro-example

“The sum of two numbers is 70. One is 8 more than the other. Smaller?” → (x + (x+8) = 70 \Rightarrow x = 31).

### Try it

The sum of two numbers is 40. One is 6 greater than the other. What is the smaller number?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Let (x) = smaller, larger = (x+6): (x + (x+6) = 40 \Rightarrow x = 17). **17.**

</details>

### Common wording to recognize

* sum of two numbers · one exceeds the other by… · twice a number · three times a number · consecutive integers

---

## Type 2 — Grouping / room / seating / packing 🪑

### Visual

47 people, rooms of 6:

[
47 \div 6 = 7 \text{ remainder } 5
]

* 7 full rooms of 6  
* **5** people in the room that is **not full**

### Key trick / translation

If they ask **“how many in the room (or row, or stack) that is not full?”** → you want the **remainder**, not the quotient.

### Try it

38 students, rooms of 5. How many in the room that is not full?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

(38 \div 5 = 7) r**3** → **3**.

</details>

---

## Type 3 — Trips / vehicles / capacity 🚌

### Visual

104 people, 42 per trip: (104 \div 42 = 2) r20 → 2 trips move 84; **20 still need a ride** → **3 trips**.

### Key trick / translation

“How many buses / trips / vans / boxes **needed**?” → divide; **any positive remainder ⇒ add one** whole container.

### Try it

61 crates, truck carries 15 per trip. How many **trips**?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

(61 \div 15 = 4) r1 → need **5** trips.

</details>

---

## Type 4 — Insufficient info / cannot be determined ❓

### Visual

Stories that **don’t pin down which quantity** you must report (e.g. “angle (B)” with no diagram) → do **not** force a number.

### Key trick / translation

> **⚠️ Trap — determinate wording:** “What are the **two angle measures** in a parallelogram if one angle is 70°?” is usually **determinate**: adjacent angles sum to 180°, opposite angles match → the two **distinct** measures are **70° and 110°**.

> **⚠️ Trap — CBD wording:** “What is **the measure of angle (B)**?” with **no labeled figure** can be *cannot be determined* — (B) might be 70° **or** 110° depending on placement.

### Worked micro-example (determinate)

One angle of a parallelogram is 70°. The four angles are (70^\circ, 110^\circ, 70^\circ, 110^\circ). The **two distinct measures** are (\boxed{70^\circ \text{ and } 110^\circ}).

### Try it

True or false: *Every* parallelogram story must avoid “cannot be determined.”

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**False.** Use CBD when the **question target** or **labels** are ambiguous (see traps above).

</details>

<a id="translation-dictionary"></a>

# 5. TRANSLATION DICTIONARY 📖

These phrases show up again and again.

## Basic word-to-math translation

* sum → add
* total → add
* more than → add
* less than → subtract
* difference → subtract
* product → multiply
* of → multiply
* quotient → divide
* per → divide / rate
* is → equals

---

## Important order traps

### “8 more than a number”

[
x + 8
]

### “8 less than a number”

[
x - 8
]

### “The difference between a number and 8”

[
x - 8
]

### “8 less than twice a number”

[
2x - 8
]

Be careful:
“less than” often reverses natural reading.

### Example

“5 less than a number” = (x - 5)
not (5 - x)

### Example

“5 less than twice a number” = (2x - 5)

<a id="principles-you-must-know"></a>

# 6. PRINCIPLES YOU MUST KNOW 🧭

These four ideas are the **same moves** as Type 1–4 in the pattern map — stated here as **rules of thumb** so you can skim before a test.

---

## Principle 1: Define the variable from the simpler quantity (Type 1)

If two numbers are related, define the smaller or simpler one first — see **Type 1**.

---

## Principle 2: Remainder means leftover (Type 2)

Division with a remainder: the **leftover** answers “incomplete room,” “partial group,” etc. — see **Type 2**.

---

## Principle 3: Trips and containers usually round up (Type 3)

If anything is left after “full loads,” you need **one more** bus/trip/box — see **Type 3**.

---

## Principle 4: Some questions do not have enough information (Type 4)

Do not invent a diagram. If the **target** is ambiguous, **cannot be determined** — see **Type 4** callouts.

<a id="fully-worked-examples"></a>

# 7. FULLY WORKED EXAMPLES ✍️

---

## Example 1 — Sum of two numbers

### Problem

The sum of two numbers is 70. One number is 8 more than the other. What is the smaller number?

### Step 1: Define the unknown

Let:
[
x = \text{smaller number}
]

Then:
[
x+8 = \text{larger number}
]

### Step 2: Write the sum equation

[
x + (x+8) = 70
]

### Step 3: Solve

[
2x + 8 = 70
]

[
2x = 62
]

[
x = 31
]

### Answer

[
\boxed{31}
]

### Check

Smaller = 31
Larger = 39
Sum = 70

Correct.

---

## Example 2 — Room not full

### Problem

There are 47 convention participants staying in the university dormitory. If one dormitory room accommodates 6 participants, how many participants will be staying in the room that is not full?

### Step 1: What are they asking?

Not the number of rooms.
They want the number in the room that is **not full**.

### Step 2: Divide

[
47 \div 6 = 7r5
]

### Step 3: Interpret

* 7 full rooms
* 5 participants in the last incomplete room

### Answer

[
\boxed{5}
]

### Logic

The remainder tells you how many are left after making as many full rooms as possible.

---

## Example 3 — Trips required

### Problem

42 passengers fit on my plane. How many trips will I have to make to transport 104 people 100 miles in 1 day?

### Step 1: Focus on the useful information

Capacity = 42 per trip
Total people = 104

The 100 miles and 1 day are distractors unless speed/time limits are involved.
Here they do not affect the number of trips.

### Step 2: Divide

[
104 \div 42 = 2r20
]

2 trips move:
[
2 \times 42 = 84
]

20 people still remain.

### Step 3: Round up

Need one more trip.

### Answer

[
\boxed{3}
]

### Logic

Trips must be whole numbers.
If even 1 person remains, another trip is needed.

<a id="the-difference-between-remainder-vs-round-up"></a>

# 8. THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN REMAINDER VS ROUND UP 🧮

This is one of the most important AR distinctions.

> **⚠️ Trap:** Same division (e.g. (38 \div 5 = 7) r3) — **“how many in the last partial room?”** = **3**; **“how many rooms needed?”** = **8**.

## Case A — Answer is the remainder

When they ask:

* how many are left?
* how many remain?
* how many in the last room?
* how many will not fit evenly?

Use the **remainder**.

### Example

38 students, rooms hold 5. How many in the room that is not full?

[
38 \div 5 = 7r3
]

Answer:
[
\boxed{3}
]

---

## Case B — Answer is round up

When they ask:

* how many buses?
* how many trips?
* how many boxes?
* how many tables?
* how many rooms are needed?

Use **quotient + 1** if remainder exists.

### Example

38 students, 5 per room. How many rooms needed?

[
38 \div 5 = 7r3
]

7 rooms hold only 35 students.
Need 1 more room.

Answer:
[
\boxed{8}
]

---

<a id="distractor-information"></a>

# 9. DISTRACTOR INFORMATION 🎭

ASVAB loves adding extra details.

Example:

“42 passengers fit on my plane. How many trips will I have to make to transport 104 people 100 miles in 1 day?”

The “100 miles in 1 day” sounds important.
But for the question asked, it is irrelevant.

So always ask:

## “Does this number actually affect what they are asking?”

If not, ignore it.

This is part of test skill.

---

<a id="cannot-be-determined"></a>

# 10. CANNOT BE DETERMINED — HOW TO SPOT IT 🚩

You should think:

### “Do I have enough information for one exact answer?”

If no, do not invent assumptions.

### Example (determinate — two distinct measures)

A parallelogram has one angle measuring 70°. **What are the two angle measures?**

Opposite angles match; adjacent angles sum to 180°. So the two **distinct** measures are always 70° and 110°.

[
\boxed{70^\circ \text{ and } 110^\circ}
]

### When to choose **cannot be determined**

Use CBD when the problem asks for a **specific named part** (e.g. angle (B), side (XY)) **without** a labeled diagram — you cannot be sure **which** of the valid measures is being requested.  
(This matches **Type 4** in the map.)

# 15. PATTERN-FIRST QUIZ 🧠

**How to use:** For each item, pick the **pattern first** (A–D), then solve. Same four types as the map: **A** = number relationships, **B** = grouping / remainder, **C** = capacity / round up, **D** = cannot be determined / ambiguous target.

---

### Q1

The sum of two numbers is 48. One number is 12 more than the other. What is the smaller number?

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**A** (two-number setup). (x + (x+12) = 48 \Rightarrow x = 18). **18.**

</details>

---

### Q2

71 participants are assigned to meeting rooms that hold 9 each. How many participants are in the room that is **not full**?

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**B** (remainder in partial group). (71 \div 9 = 7) r**8**. **8.**

</details>

---

### Q3

Same 71 participants, rooms of 9. **How many rooms are needed** to seat everyone?

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**C** (round up). (71 \div 9 = 7) r8 → **8** rooms.

</details>

---

### Q4

A shuttle holds 25 people. How many **round trips** are needed to move **80** people if every trip is full except possibly the last?

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**C**. (80 \div 25 = 3) r5 → **4** trips.

</details>

---

### Q5

The difference between two numbers is 9. Their sum is 45. What is the **larger** number?

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**A**. Smaller (x), larger (x+9): (x + (x+9) = 45 \Rightarrow x = 18), larger = **27**.

</details>

### Q6

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**D** — **cannot be determined**. Without a diagram, (B) could be 55° or 125°.

</details>

### Q7

“A van holds 14 crates. How many trips to deliver **76** crates **across town 30 miles**?” (Assume distance does not limit load.)

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**C** — “30 miles” is a distractor. (76 \div 14 = 5) r6 → **6** trips.

</details>

### Q8

* **Pattern:** A · B · C · D  
* Your answer:

<details>
<summary>Reveal pattern + answer</summary>

**A** (translate to (3x - 4 = 20 \Rightarrow x = 8)). **8.**

</details>

### Q9

<details>

* Your answer:

<details>

**A** — treat like a **known-relationship** item (parallelogram angle facts), **not** remainder/round-up and **not** CBD. The two distinct measures are **110° and 70°**.

</details>

<a id="speed-recognition-drill"></a>

# 16. SPEED RECOGNITION DRILL ⚡

Rapid checks — **under ~10 seconds** each. Say “remainder,” “round up,” “equation,” or “CBD” before you calculate.

---

1. 90 people, lifeboats hold 20, **how many people** in the last **partial** boat?  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **Remainder** → (90 \div 20 = 4) r**10**.</details>

2. Same setup; **how many boats** for everyone?  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **Round up** → **5** boats.</details>

3. “Sum is 60; one is 14 more than the other — smaller?”  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **Equation / Type 1** → (x + (x+14) = 60 \Rightarrow x = 23).</details>

4. “Twice a number **plus** 7 equals 21.”  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **Equation** → (2x+7=21 \Rightarrow x=7).</details>

5. Extra sentence: “The mission is **urgent**.” The math asks only for total buses.  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **Distractor wording** — ignore; classify by numbers given.</details>

6. “What is angle (X)?” Parallelogram, one angle 40°, **no diagram.**  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **CBD** for the **named** angle without a figure.</details>

7. 100 miles vs 100 people — which tells you trip count for a bus?  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **People ÷ capacity**; miles irrelevant unless a rate limit is stated.</details>

8. 41 pencils, boxes of 12, **boxes needed** for **all** pencils.  
<details><summary>Reveal</summary> **Round up** → (41 \div 12 = 3) r5 → **4** boxes.</details>

## Test Question 1

The sum of two numbers is 70. One number is 8 more than the other. What's the smaller number?

### Think

“smaller number” is the unknown.

Let:
[
x = \text{smaller}
]

Then:
[
x+8 = \text{larger}
]

The sum is 70:

[
x + (x+8) = 70
]

[
2x+8=70
]

[
2x=62
]

[
x=31
]

### Answer

[
\boxed{31}
]

## Test Question 2

There are 47 convention participants staying in the university dormitory. If one dormitory room accommodates 6 participants, how many participants will be staying in the room that is not full?

### Think

This is a **grouping / remainder** question.

Not asking:

* how many rooms total

It is asking:

* how many in the incomplete room

So divide:

[
47 \div 6 = 7r5
]

Interpretation:

* 7 rooms are full
* 5 are left for the room that is not full

### Answer

[
\boxed{5}
]

## Test Question 3

42 passengers fit on my plane. How many trips will I have to make to transport 104 people 100 miles in 1 day?

### Think

Important info:

* 42 per trip
* 104 total people

Extra info:

* 100 miles
* 1 day

Those do not affect trip count unless speed/time limits are given.

Now divide:

[
104 \div 42 = 2r20
]

2 trips move only 84 people.
20 still remain.
Need one more trip.

### Answer

[
\boxed{3}
]

<a id="quick-recognition"></a>

# 18. QUICK RECOGNITION GUIDE 👀

When you see this wording, think this:

### “sum of two numbers”

→ equation with addition

### “one is 8 more than the other”

→ (x) and (x+8)

### “how many in the room that is not full”

→ divide and use remainder

### “how many trips/buses/boxes/rooms needed”

→ divide and round up

### “which two angles?” (in a parallelogram, distinct measures)

→ usually **70° and 110°** style (determinate) unless wording asks a **named** angle with **no** diagram → **CBD**

### extra details like “100 miles in 1 day”

→ often **distractors** when the question is only about grouping or counts

<a id="common-mistakes"></a>

# 19. COMMON MISTAKES ⚠️

> **⚠️ Trap:** The fastest errors here are picking the **quotient** when the story wants the **remainder**, and picking the **remainder** when the story wants **one more bus/trip**.

## Mistake 1: Using quotient when you need remainder

Example:
47 people, rooms of 6, how many in the room not full?

Wrong:
7

Why wrong?
7 is the number of full rooms, not the people in the incomplete room.

Correct:
5

---

## Mistake 2: Using remainder when you need round up

Example:
65 people, buses hold 12, how many buses?

Wrong:
5 remainder 5, so answer 5

Why wrong?
5 buses only carry 60 people.

Correct:
6

---

## Mistake 3: Not defining the variable clearly

If you do not know whether (x) is smaller or larger, the setup gets sloppy.

---

## Mistake 4: Letting distractor information confuse you

Only use what affects the question asked.

---

## Mistake 5: Forcing an answer when information is insufficient

Sometimes the right answer really is:
[
\boxed{\text{cannot be determined}}
]

# 20. FINAL MASTER SUMMARY 🎓

## For word problems on the ASVAB:

### 1. Identify what is being asked

What exactly do they want?

### 2. Define the unknown

Let (x) be the quantity asked for, or the simplest quantity.

### 3. Translate words into math

* sum → add
* more than → add
* less than → subtract
* per → divide/rate
* is → equals

### 4. Solve

Use arithmetic or simple algebra.

### 5. Interpret the answer

* remainder if asking for leftover
* round up if asking for trips/containers needed
* cannot be determined if info is incomplete
