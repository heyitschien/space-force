### Understanding Voltage, Current, Resistance, and Power

<div style="background: #ffebee; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #f44336; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #c62828; margin-top: 0;">üî¥ The Four Fundamental Quantities</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Every electrical circuit involves four key quantities:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Voltage (V)</strong> - Electrical "pressure" that pushes electrons</li>
    <li><strong>Current (I)</strong> - Flow of electrons through a circuit</li>
    <li><strong>Resistance (R)</strong> - Opposition to electron flow</li>
    <li><strong>Power (P)</strong> - Rate at which electrical energy is used</li>
  </ul>
</div>

### Voltage: Electrical Pressure

<div style="background: #e8f5e9; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #4caf50; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #2e7d32; margin-top: 0;">üü¢ What Voltage Is</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Voltage</strong> (also called "electrical potential difference") is the force that pushes electrons through a circuit.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Think of voltage like water pressure in a hose - higher pressure pushes water harder.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Unit:</strong> Volts (V)</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Symbol:</strong> V or E</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Common Values:</strong> AA battery = 1.5V, Car battery = 12V, Wall outlet = 120V</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** Voltage is measured between two points. A battery creates voltage by having a positive terminal (high potential) and negative terminal (low potential).

### Current: Electron Flow

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">üîµ What Current Is</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Current</strong> is the flow of electrons through a circuit.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Think of current like the flow rate of water - how many gallons per minute flow through a pipe.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Unit:</strong> Amperes or Amps (A)</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Symbol:</strong> I</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Common Values:</strong> Small LED = 0.02A, Light bulb = 0.5A, Car starter = 100A+</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** Current flows when there's a complete circuit path and voltage to push it. No voltage = no current.

### Resistance: Opposition to Flow

<div style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #ff9800; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #e65100; margin-top: 0;">üü† What Resistance Is</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Resistance</strong> opposes the flow of current.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Think of resistance like a narrow pipe - it restricts water flow.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Unit:</strong> Ohms (Œ©)</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Symbol:</strong> R</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Common Values:</strong> Wire = very low (0.01Œ©), Light bulb = 240Œ©, Open circuit = infinite</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** Higher resistance means less current flows for the same voltage. Resistors are components designed to create resistance.

### Power: Energy Usage Rate

<div style="background: #f3e5f5; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #9c27b0; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #7b1fa2; margin-top: 0;">üü£ What Power Is</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Power</strong> is the rate at which electrical energy is converted to other forms (light, heat, motion).</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Think of power like horsepower - how much work can be done per second.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Unit:</strong> Watts (W)</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Symbol:</strong> P</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Common Values:</strong> LED bulb = 10W, Incandescent bulb = 60W, Hair dryer = 1500W</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** Power tells you how much energy a device uses. Higher power = more energy consumed.

### The Relationship Between Concepts

<div style="background: #e1f5fe; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #0277bd; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #0277bd; margin-top: 0;">üîµ How They Connect</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Voltage</strong> pushes <strong>current</strong> through <strong>resistance</strong>, creating <strong>power</strong>.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">These relationships are described by formulas:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Ohm's Law:</strong> V = I √ó R (voltage = current √ó resistance)</li>
    <li><strong>Power Formula:</strong> P = I √ó V (power = current √ó voltage)</li>
  </ul>
</div>

## 2Ô∏è‚É£ Ohm's Law Mastery

### The Fundamental Relationship

<div style="background: #ffebee; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #f44336; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #c62828; margin-top: 0;">üî¥ Critical Formula: Ohm's Law</h3>
  <p style="font-size: 1.4em; margin: 10px 0; text-align: center;"><strong>V = I √ó R</strong></p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0; text-align: center;">Voltage = Current √ó Resistance</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Units:</strong> Volts (V) = Amperes (A) √ó Ohms (Œ©)</p>
</div>

**What This Means:** Ohm's Law describes the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance. If you know any two values, you can calculate the third.

### All Formula Variations

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">üîµ Three Forms of Ohm's Law</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Starting from V = I √ó R, you can rearrange to solve for any variable:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>V = I √ó R</strong> - Find voltage when you know current and resistance</li>
    <li><strong>I = V √∑ R</strong> - Find current when you know voltage and resistance</li>
    <li><strong>R = V √∑ I</strong> - Find resistance when you know voltage and current</li>
  </ul>
</div>

**Memory Tip:** Use the triangle method - cover the variable you want to find:
- Cover V ‚Üí I √ó R remains
- Cover I ‚Üí V √∑ R remains  
- Cover R ‚Üí V √∑ I remains

### When to Use Each Form

<div style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #ff9800; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #e65100; margin-top: 0;">üü† Choosing the Right Formula</h3>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Use V = I √ó R</strong> when you need to find voltage</li>
    <li><strong>Use I = V √∑ R</strong> when you need to find current</li>
    <li><strong>Use R = V √∑ I</strong> when you need to find resistance</li>
  </ul>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Key:</strong> Look at what the problem asks for, then use the formula that solves for that variable.</p>
</div>

#### Example 1: Finding Voltage (V = I √ó R)

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h4 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">Problem</h4>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">A circuit has a current of 2 amperes flowing through a resistor of 50 ohms. What is the voltage across the resistor?</p>
</div>

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify what you're looking for
- We need to find voltage (V)

**Step 2:** Identify what you know
- Current (I) = 2 amperes
- Resistance (R) = 50 ohms

**Step 3:** Choose the correct formula
- Since we're finding V, use: V = I √ó R

**Step 4:** Substitute the values
- V = 2 √ó 50

**Step 5:** Calculate
- V = 100

**Step 6:** Add units
- V = 100 volts

**Answer:** 100 volts

**Why This Works:** Ohm's Law directly relates voltage to current and resistance. When current flows through resistance, voltage is created.

  <p><strong>10. Ohm's law states that voltage equals what?</strong></p>
  <p><strong>Answer:</strong> Voltage = Current √ó Resistance (V = I √ó R)</p>
  
  <p><strong>11. If you know voltage and current, how do you find resistance?</strong></p>
  <p><strong>Answer:</strong> R = V √∑ I (resistance equals voltage divided by current)</p>
  
  <p><strong>12. If you know current and resistance, how do you find voltage?</strong></p>

## 3Ô∏è‚É£ Power Formulas

### Understanding Power Calculations

<div style="background: #ffebee; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #f44336; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #c62828; margin-top: 0;">üî¥ Power: Energy Per Unit Time</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Power</strong> tells you how fast electrical energy is converted to other forms (light, heat, motion).</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Think of power like speed - it's a rate. Just as speed is distance per time, power is energy per time.</p>
</div>

### Three Power Formulas

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">üîµ All Ways to Calculate Power</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">You can calculate power using any two of: current, voltage, or resistance:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>P = I √ó V</strong> - Power = Current √ó Voltage (most common)</li>
    <li><strong>P = I¬≤ √ó R</strong> - Power = Current¬≤ √ó Resistance</li>
    <li><strong>P = V¬≤ √∑ R</strong> - Power = Voltage¬≤ √∑ Resistance</li>
  </ul>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Unit:</strong> Watts (W)</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** All three formulas give the same answer. Use whichever formula has the values you know.

### When to Use Each Formula

<div style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #ff9800; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #e65100; margin-top: 0;">üü† Choosing the Right Formula</h3>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Use P = I √ó V</strong> when you know current and voltage</li>
    <li><strong>Use P = I¬≤ √ó R</strong> when you know current and resistance</li>
    <li><strong>Use P = V¬≤ √∑ R</strong> when you know voltage and resistance</li>
  </ul>
</div>

### Rearranging Power Formulas

<div style="background: #e8f5e9; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #4caf50; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #2e7d32; margin-top: 0;">üü¢ Finding Other Values</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">From P = I √ó V, you can find:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>I = P √∑ V</strong> - Current = Power √∑ Voltage</li>
    <li><strong>V = P √∑ I</strong> - Voltage = Power √∑ Current</li>
  </ul>
</div>

---

#### Example 1: Basic Power Calculation (P = I √ó V)

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">

**Step 1:** Identify what you're looking for
- We need to find power (P)

**Step 2:** Identify what you know
- Current (I) = 0.5 amperes
- Voltage (V) = 120 volts

**Step 3:** Choose the correct formula
- Since we know I and V, use: P = I √ó V

**Step 4:** Substitute the values
- P = 0.5 √ó 120

**Step 5:** Calculate
- P = 60

**Step 6:** Add units
- P = 60 watts

**Answer:** 60 watts

**Why This Works:** Power is current times voltage. This tells you how much energy the bulb converts per second.

### How to Read 4-Band Resistors

<div style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #ff9800; margin: 15px 0;">

    <li><strong>Band 4:</strong> Tolerance (usually gold ¬±5% or silver ¬±10%)</li>
  </ol>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Formula:</strong> Value = (Band1 Band2) √ó Multiplier</p>

#### Example 1: Brown-Black-Red Resistor

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">

  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">A resistor's first three color bands are brown, black, and red. What is its value?</p>
</div>

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify Band 1 (first digit)
- Brown = 1

**Step 2:** Identify Band 2 (second digit)
- Black = 0

**Step 3:** Identify Band 3 (multiplier)
- Red = √ó100

**Step 4:** Combine digits
- Band1-Band2 = 1-0 = 10

**Step 5:** Multiply by multiplier
- 10 √ó 100 = 1,000 ohms

**Answer:** 1,000 ohms (or 1 kilohm)

**Why This Works:** The first two bands give you the base number (10), and the third band tells you how many zeros to add (√ó100 means add 2 zeros).

## 5Ô∏è‚É£ AC vs DC Fundamentals

### Understanding Current Types

<div style="background: #e8f5e9; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #4caf50; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #2e7d32; margin-top: 0;">üü¢ Two Types of Current</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">There are two fundamental ways electrons can flow in a circuit:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>DC (Direct Current)</strong> - Electrons flow in one direction only</li>
    <li><strong>AC (Alternating Current)</strong> - Electrons reverse direction periodically</li>
  </ul>
</div>

### Direct Current (DC)

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">üîµ What DC Is</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Direct Current (DC)</strong> flows in one direction only, like water flowing downhill.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Common Sources:</strong></p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li>Batteries (AA, AAA, car batteries)</li>
    <li>Solar cells</li>
    <li>DC power supplies</li>
  </ul>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Visual:</strong> A straight line on a graph (constant voltage)</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** DC is steady and constant. The voltage doesn't change direction.

### Alternating Current (AC)

<div style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #ff9800; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #e65100; margin-top: 0;">üü† What AC Is</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Alternating Current (AC)</strong> reverses direction periodically, like a pendulum swinging back and forth.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Common Sources:</strong></p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li>Wall outlets (120V AC in US)</li>
    <li>Generators</li>
    <li>Power plants</li>
  </ul>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Visual:</strong> A sine wave on a graph (voltage goes positive, then negative, repeating)</p>
</div>

**Key Concept:** AC changes direction 60 times per second (60 Hz) in US power systems.

### Rectification: Converting AC to DC

<div style="background: #f3e5f5; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #9c27b0; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #7b1fa2; margin-top: 0;">üü£ Changing AC to DC</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Rectification</strong> is the process of converting AC to DC.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">This is done using diodes, which allow current in only one direction.</p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;"><strong>Why needed:</strong> Many electronic devices (phones, computers) need DC, but wall outlets provide AC.</p>
</div>

## 7Ô∏è‚É£ Series and Parallel Circuits

### Circuit Types

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">üîµ Two Basic Circuit Configurations</h3>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Components can be connected in two ways:</p>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Series:</strong> Components connected end-to-end (one path)</li>
    <li><strong>Parallel:</strong> Components connected side-by-side (multiple paths)</li>
  </ul>
</div>

### Series Circuits

<div style="background: #ffebee; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #f44336; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #c62828; margin-top: 0;">üî¥ Series Circuit Rules</h3>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Current:</strong> Same through all components (I_total = I1 = I2 = I3...)</li>
    <li><strong>Voltage:</strong> Divides across components (V_total = V1 + V2 + V3...)</li>
    <li><strong>Resistance:</strong> Adds up (R_total = R1 + R2 + R3...)</li>
  </ul>
</div>

**Key Concept:** In series, current has only one path, so it's the same everywhere. Voltage divides.

### Parallel Circuits

<div style="background: #e8f5e9; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #4caf50; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #2e7d32; margin-top: 0;">üü¢ Parallel Circuit Rules</h3>
  <ul style="line-height: 1.8;">
    <li><strong>Voltage:</strong> Same across all components (V_total = V1 = V2 = V3...)</li>
    <li><strong>Current:</strong> Divides among paths (I_total = I1 + I2 + I3...)</li>
    <li><strong>Resistance:</strong> 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3...</li>
  </ul>
</div>

**Key Concept:** In parallel, voltage is the same across all branches. Current divides among paths.

### Series Resistance Formula

<div style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #ff9800; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #e65100; margin-top: 0;">üü† Calculating Series Resistance</h3>
  <p style="font-size: 1.2em; margin: 10px 0;"><strong>R_total = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...</strong></p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Simply add all resistances together.</p>
</div>

### Parallel Resistance Formula

<div style="background: #f3e5f5; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #9c27b0; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h3 style="color: #7b1fa2; margin-top: 0;">üü£ Calculating Parallel Resistance</h3>
  <p style="font-size: 1.2em; margin: 10px 0;"><strong>1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...</strong></p>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">For two resistors: R_total = (R1 √ó R2) √∑ (R1 + R2)</p>
</div>

#### Example 1: Series Resistance

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">
  <h4 style="color: #1976d2; margin-top: 0;">Problem</h4>
  <p style="margin: 10px 0;">Three resistors of 10Œ©, 20Œ©, and 30Œ© are connected in series. What is the total resistance?</p>
</div>

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify circuit type
- Series circuit

**Step 2:** Use series formula
- R_total = R1 + R2 + R3

**Step 3:** Substitute values
- R_total = 10 + 20 + 30

**Step 4:** Calculate
- R_total = 60 ohms

**Answer:** 60 ohms

**Why This Works:** In series, resistances add directly because current must flow through each resistor.

#### Example 2: Parallel Resistance (Two Resistors)

<div style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 15px; border-radius: 8px; border-left: 5px solid #2196f3; margin: 15px 0;">

**Step 1:** Identify circuit type
- Parallel circuit

**Step 2:** Use parallel formula for two resistors
- R_total = (R1 √ó R2) √∑ (R1 + R2)

**Step 3:** Substitute values
- R_total = (6 √ó 12) √∑ (6 + 12)

**Step 4:** Calculate numerator
- 6 √ó 12 = 72

**Step 5:** Calculate denominator
- 6 + 12 = 18

**Step 6:** Divide
- R_total = 72 √∑ 18 = 4 ohms

**Why This Works:** In parallel, total resistance is less than the smallest resistor because current has multiple paths.

  <p><strong>Solution:</strong> R_total = (8 √ó 8) √∑ (8 + 8) = 64 √∑ 16 = 4 ohms</p>
  
  <p><strong>3. In a series circuit, is current the same or different through each component?</strong></p>
