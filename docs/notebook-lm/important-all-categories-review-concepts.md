To achieve a perfect score on these specific practice exams—and comfortably exceed the 80+ Electrical (E) and 75+ General (G) composite targets for technical roles—you must master the exact mechanics, vocabulary, and formulas tested within them.

Here is the detailed breakdown of every concept, idea, and principle you need to study, categorized by subtest.

### 1. General Science (GS)

This section requires rote memorization of foundational biology, chemistry, earth science, and physics.

* **Cellular Biology & Human Anatomy:** * Cell division processes (mitosis vs. meiosis) and embryology (cleavage).
* Cellular anatomy (nucleus, mitochondria, protoplasm, and water as the primary inorganic substance).
* Human systems: Nervous system (spinal cord, brainstem functions), circulatory system (heart chambers, specifically the right ventricle pumping to the lungs, red vs. white blood cell functions), and the digestive system.
* Genetics (genes, chromosomes, DNA).


* **Taxonomy & Ecology:**
* Classification hierarchies (Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species).
* Animal diets (carnivore, herbivore, omnivore) and food chain roles (producers vs. consumers).
* Biomes (e.g., tropical rainforest characteristics).


* **Earth & Space Science:**
* Planetary facts (order from the sun, Mars' atmosphere, planets with rings, largest moons like Ganymede/Titan).
* Stellar life cycles (black holes, yellow stars) and proximity (Proxima Centauri).
* Earth's layers (crust, mantle, core) and atmospheric/biospheric definitions.
* Moon phases and phenomena (lunar/solar eclipses, "blue moons").
* Rock types (igneous formation, examples like pumice/shale).


* **Chemistry & Physics Fundamentals:**
* Atomic structure (protons determine the atomic number, electrons are negative and orbit).
* States of matter (melting, boiling, freezing points) and the Celsius scale.
* pH scale (acids taste sour, pH < 7).
* Newton’s Laws of Motion (inertia, acceleration proportional to mass), vector quantities (velocity), and energy/power definitions (measured in Joules and Watts).
* Speed of light waves.



### 2. Arithmetic Reasoning (AR)

This section tests your ability to extract mathematical equations from real-world word problems without a calculator.

* **Rate, Distance, and Time:** Mastering the $d = rt$ (distance = rate $\times$ time) formula. You must be able to calculate average speeds, intercept times (e.g., Car A leaving 30 mins before Car B), and fuel efficiency (miles per gallon/cost per trip).
* **Percentages & Financial Math:** Calculating discounts, taxes, interest ($Interest = Principal \times Rate \times Time$), tip percentages, and wage increases.
* **Fractions & Ratios:** Finding common denominators to add/subtract fractions (e.g., portions of pies eaten), scaling ratios, and converting mixed numbers into improper fractions.
* **Geometry Application:** Calculating the area of 2D spaces (length $\times$ width) to find material costs (like carpeting priced per square yard vs. square foot).
* **Unit Conversions:** Flawlessly converting hours to minutes, tons to pounds to ounces, gallons to quarts, and feet to inches.

### 3. Word Knowledge (WK)

To 100% this section, you must expand your vocabulary to identify precise synonyms and antonyms. You need to recognize context clues to deduce meanings.

* **Core Vocabulary Found in These Tests:** *Assimulate, theorize, symmetrical, proficient, goad, headlong, flagrant, legacy, unconscionable, laudable, burnished, oblique, superfluous, hypocrisy, hemorrhage, garrison, fiscal, domicile, abate, succinct, demise, charisma, burrow, prevail, contravene, chasm, fundamental, susceptible, emit, apex, brittle, erratic, fanatic, fallible, atone, frail, feeble, mundane, convert, commend, forthright, autocrat, fanciful, boorish, forerunner, tenable, asperity, incalculable.*
* **Strategy:** Study Greek and Latin root words, prefixes, and suffixes. Knowing that "auto-" means self and "-crat" means rule helps deduce that *autocrat* means a dictator, even if you haven't seen the word before.

### 4. Paragraph Comprehension (PC)

* **Information Extraction:** Pinpointing explicit details hidden within a text (e.g., exactly how many senators exist, or what year a specific radio was invented).
* **Main Idea:** Distinguishing the central thesis of a paragraph from its supporting details (e.g., selecting the best title for a passage).
* **Inference:** Drawing logical, unstated conclusions based *only* on the provided text, without bringing in outside assumptions.

### 5. Mathematics Knowledge (MK)

This is pure, abstract mathematics.

* **Algebraic Operations:** * Isolating variables in linear equations and inequalities (e.g., solving for $x$ in $2+x \ge 4$).
* Evaluating functions by substituting values.
* Exponent rules: Multiplying variables with exponents ($x^3 \cdot x^4 = x^7$) and powers of powers.
* Expanding polynomials using the FOIL method (First, Outside, Inside, Last) for equations like $(x+4)(x+2)$.
* Calculating factorials (e.g., $5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1$).


* **Geometry & Trigonometry:**
* Properties of shapes: Triangles (equilateral, right, isosceles), quadrilaterals (parallelograms, trapezoids, rhombuses), and the sum of their interior angles (triangles = $180^\circ$, quadrilaterals = $360^\circ$).
* Circle formulas: Circumference ($C = \pi d$) and Area ($A = \pi r^2$).
* Volume formulas for 3D boxes ($V = lwh$).
* Coordinate geometry: Finding the equation of a line ($y = mx + b$) given two points on a graph.


* **Number Theory:** Identifying prime numbers, prime factorizations, and statistical concepts like mode, median, and mean.

### 6. Electronics Information (EI)

* **Electrical Laws & Formulas:** Complete mastery of Ohm's Law ($V = I \times R$) and the Power formula ($P = I \times V$). Know that power is measured in Watts, current in Amperes, potential difference in Volts, and resistance in Ohms.
* **Circuits & AC/DC:** Understanding the difference between alternating current (AC) and direct current (DC), and the process of rectification (converting AC to DC).
* **Component Identification:** Knowing the symbols and functions of resistors, capacitors (and how they behave in parallel vs. series), inductors (measured in millihenries), diodes (bridge rectifiers), and transformers.
* **Hardware & Wiring:** Wire gauge sizing (smaller numbers indicate larger diameter wires), neutral wire coloring (white/natural), grounding/bonding principles, and testing tools (ammeters, voltmeters).
* **Frequencies:** Understanding Hertz (cycles per second), radio waves (which travel at the speed of light), and radar operating frequencies.

### 7. Auto & Shop Information (AS)

* **Engine Anatomy:** The 4-stroke cycle (intake, compression, power, exhaust), the difference between single-acting and opposed piston engines, and identifying components like the camshaft, crankshaft, connecting rods, and piston rings.
* **Automotive Systems:** * *Ignition:* Electronic vs. conventional systems, alternators, voltage regulators.
* *Cooling:* Water pumps and the dangers of pouring cold water on a hot engine block.
* *Drivetrain/Brakes:* Limited-slip differentials, master cylinders, hydraulic line troubleshooting (e.g., air causing a soft pedal).


* **Tools & Shop Practices:**
* Identifying tools visually and by function: Coping saws, hacksaws, miter box back-saws, ripsaws (cutting with the grain).
* Wrenches (box vs. open-ended, strap/chain wrenches for pipes).
* Measurement (calipers, depth gages, plumb-bobs for vertical reference, levels for horizontal trueness).
* Hardware (bucking bars for rivets, nail sets, types of washers to prevent slipping, rebar for concrete).



### 8. Mechanical Comprehension (MC)

* **Physics of Force & Pressure:** Calculating pressure applied to a surface area ($P = F / A$, or psi of liquids based on depth).
* **Gears & Pulleys:** Calculating gear ratios. If Gear A has 25 teeth and Gear B has 15, you must know how to calculate their rotational relationship. Know that meshed gears turn in opposite directions.
* **Levers & Mechanical Advantage:** Identifying the fulcrum, effort arm, and resistance arm. You must be able to calculate the mechanical advantage of block-and-tackle pulley systems (by counting the supporting ropes).
* **Mechanisms & Thermodynamics:** How induction/friction clutches work, fluid dynamics (valves and drainage), thermal conductivity (which metals heat up fastest), and the physics of momentum and recoil.

### 9. Assembling Objects (AO)

* **Spatial Reasoning:** Mentally translating flat, 2D pieces and visualizing how they connect at specific points to form a complete shape.
* **Pattern Recognition:** Identifying how lines, geometric edges, and specific joints align when rotated or flipped in a 3D space. (This requires visual practice rather than formula memorization).
