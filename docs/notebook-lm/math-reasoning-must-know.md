To excel in the Arithmetic Reasoning and Mathematics Knowledge sections of the ASVAB, you must master several core mathematical principles and formulas. Here are the fundamental concepts extracted from the practice materials:

**1. Arithmetic & Word Problem Strategies**
*   **Calculating Averages:** To find an average (or mean), add all the values together and divide the sum by the total number of values. You can also use this formula to find a missing test score needed to reach a specific average by setting up an equation with $x$ as the unknown score.
*   **Rate, Distance, and Time:** Use the formula **Distance = Speed × Time** ($d = rt$) to solve travel problems. For example, if you need to find how long it takes a driver to catch up to another, calculate the distance the first person traveled, then divide that distance by the difference in their speeds,.
*   **Simple Interest:** Use the formula **$I = Prt$** (Interest = Principal × Rate × Time in years) to determine the amount of interest earned or owed. 
*   **Fractions:** When adding or subtracting fractions, you must first convert them to a least common denominator,. 
*   **Percentages and Discounts:** To calculate a percentage increase (like a raise), multiply the original amount by the percentage, and add that number to the original amount. To convert a percentage into a fraction, place the percent value over 100 and reduce it to its simplest form.
*   **Unit Conversions:** Always use consistent units of measurement before solving an equation. You must know standard equivalents, such as: 
    *   1 yard = 3 feet
    *   1 square yard = 9 square feet
    *   1 gross = 144 items
    *   1 kilometer is approximately $\frac{5}{8}$ of a mile

**2. Algebra Principles**
*   **Setting Up Equations:** Learn to translate word problems into algebraic equations. For example, if the sum of two numbers is 70 and one is 8 more than the other, set the smaller number to $x$ and the larger to $x + 8$, making the equation $x + (x + 8) = 70$. To calculate when a business will make a profit, set up an inequality where Revenue > Cost.
*   **Multiplying Polynomials (FOIL):** Use the FOIL acronym (First, Outside, Inside, Last) to multiply binomials like $(x + 4)(x + 2)$,.
*   **Exponent Rules:** If two powers have the exact same base, you can multiply them by keeping the base the same and adding the exponents together (e.g., $x^3 \cdot x^4 = x^7$).
*   **Linear Equations ($y = mx + b$):** To find the equation of a line, first find the slope ($m$) by dividing the change in $y$ values by the change in $x$ values,. Then, substitute one of the given points into $y = mx + b$ to solve for the y-intercept ($b$).
*   **Inequalities:** Solve basic inequalities just as you would solve an equation to isolate the unknown variable,.

**3. Geometry & Measurement**
*   **Circles:** The circumference of a circle is equal to $\pi \times \text{diameter}$ ($C = \pi d$). Because the diameter is twice the radius, this can also be written as $C = 2\pi r$.
*   **Area and Volume:** The volume of a rectangular shape is **Length × Width × Height** ($V = lwh$). The area of a rectangular surface is **Length × Width**.
*   **Triangles:** An equilateral triangle is a triangle where all three sides are equal and all three angles are equal.
*   **Quadrilaterals:** All four-sided figures (quadrilaterals), such as trapezoids, have interior angles that add up to exactly 360 degrees,.
*   **Angle Rules:** **Complementary angles** are two angles whose sum equals 90 degrees. **Supplementary angles** are two angles whose sum equals 180 degrees.

**4. Number Operations & Properties**
*   **Order of Operations (PEMDAS):** Always solve the parts of an equation in parentheses first, followed by exponents/powers, then multiplication/division, and finally addition/subtraction,,,.
*   **Factorials (!):** The factorial of a number means multiplying that number by every next-smallest whole number down to 1. For instance, $5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 120$.
*   **Prime Numbers:** A prime number can only be divided evenly by itself and by 1 (e.g., 11).
*   **Mode:** In a series of numbers, the mode is the specific number that appears most frequently.
